### **1. De Morgan’s Laws**

These are explicitly identified as a key topic for statement and proof.

- **Law 1:** $\neg(p \wedge q) \equiv \neg p \vee \neg q$ (The negation of a conjunction is the disjunction of the negations).
- **Law 2:** $\neg(p \vee q) \equiv \neg p \wedge \neg q$ (The negation of a disjunction is the conjunction of the negations).

### **2. Implication Laws (Logical Equivalences)**

The sources highlight several critical equivalences used for simplifying complex propositional formulas:

- **Fundamental Implication:** $p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \vee q$.
- **Contrapositive:** $p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg q \rightarrow \neg p$.
- **Exportation Law:** $(x \wedge y) \Rightarrow z$ is logically equivalent to $x \Rightarrow (y \Rightarrow z)$.
- **Disjunctive Implication:** $(r \vee p) \rightarrow (r \vee q)$ is logically equivalent to $r \vee (p \rightarrow q)$.

### **3. The Three Rules of Inference**

While distinct from the algebraic "laws," the sources consistently require a summary of the **three rules of inference** in Propositional Logic to facilitate reasoning. Although the specific names are not listed in the snippets, these typically include:

1. **Modus Ponens:** If $P$ is true and $P \rightarrow Q$ is true, then $Q$ is true.
2. **Modus Tollens:** If $P \rightarrow Q$ is true and $Q$ is false ($\neg Q$), then $P$ is false ($\neg P$).
3. **Syllogism:** If $P \rightarrow Q$ and $Q \rightarrow R$, then $P \rightarrow R$.

### **4. Other Standard Laws**

The sources require students to be able to apply and prove standard algebraic laws of logic:

- **Commutative Laws:** $p \vee q \equiv q \vee p$ and $p \wedge q \equiv q \wedge p$.
- **Associative Laws:** $(p \vee q) \vee r \equiv p \vee (q \vee r)$.
- **Distributive Laws:** $p \wedge (q \vee r) \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)$.

### **Exam Strategy for Propositional Logic**

When asked to "state and prove" these laws, you must:

- **State the symbolic formula** clearly.
- **Construct a Truth Table** showing that for every possible combination of truth values for the variables, the final output for both sides of the equivalence is identical.
- **Apply the Deduction Theorem** for specific situational proofs, such as determining logical goals from conditional facts.
