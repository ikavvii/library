Based on the provided examination papers and syllabus, the three primary characteristics of enterprise software are:

1. **Integration of Disparate Applications:** Enterprise software is designed to consolidate separate, isolated systems into a **coherent whole** to ensure a unified IT strategy.
    
    - **Example:** A **university system** that integrates fragmented applications for accounts, academic registration, and exam marks so that students do not have to provide the same information repeatedly across different departments.
2. **Multi-tier or Multi-layer Architecture:** These applications utilize a layered structure (such as Presentation, Business Logic, and Data layers) to remain **flexible** and support various client programs handling user interactions.
    
    - **Example:** A **hospital enterprise architecture** where a multi-tier system allows different departments—like radiology, pharmacy, and billing—to access a centralized patient record database through various web or mobile interfaces.
3. **Complex Business Process Management (Complexity):** Enterprise software is built to handle massive, multi-step business logic and large-scale data processing across an entire organization.
    
    - **Example:** An **online purchase system** that manages complex workflows including real-time inventory verification, bill computation, payment processing, and coordinating shipping information.

Another recurring characteristic found in the sources is **Scalability**, which refers to the system's ability to handle millions of concurrent user interactions, such as those found in high-traffic **e-commerce platforms** or online **cab service management systems**.
